Week 7 Blog Post

Website Analysis
Reatile Davhana
May , 2025

News24 as an example. News24 is one of South Africa’s leading independent news websites, known for its broad coverage of local and international news. Below are some potential pros and cons for this website:

Pros

  • Comprehensive Coverage:
    News24 offers extensive coverage of local, regional, and international news, along with politics, sports, business, and lifestyle sections. This broad scope makes it a one-stop destination for staying informed about current events.
  • Timely Updates:
    The website is known for its rapid news updates, which is especially useful in a dynamic media environment where local and international events unfold quickly.
  • Multimedia Integration:
    It features not just written articles but also photos, videos, and infographics, enhancing user engagement and providing a richer storytelling experience.
  • User-Friendly Interface:
    With a clean layout and structured navigation, users can easily find different sections, making for a generally straightforward browsing experience.
  • Mobile Responsiveness: The site is optimized for mobile devices, ensuring that it scales well across different screen sizes—a critical aspect considering the increasing use of smartphones for accessing news

Cons

  • Advertising Clutter:
    Like many free news sites, News24 relies on advertisements, which can sometimes be intrusive or clutter the reading experience if not managed effectively.
  • Content Overload:
    The breadth of content can sometimes feel overwhelming. Casual readers may find the volume of information difficult to sift through quickly, especially during major breaking news events.
  • Load Times with Multimedia:
    While multimedia elements enhance the experience, users on slower internet connections may experience longer load times, which can affect accessibility in certain areas.
  • Complexity for First-Time Users:
    The abundance of sections and detailed coverage might intimidate new visitors who are looking for quick headlines rather than in-depth analysis.

This assessment is based on a general analysis of its features and user experiences; personal preferences and needs might lead to different evaluations for different readers.

Sambuli defines digital equality as striving for equality in the digital age, a goal that remains elusive for many. She emphasizes that achieving digital equality involves examining both analog and digital aspects of political, social, cultural, and economic dimensions in society. The aim is to ensure that digital technologies do not exacerbate existing inequalities.

Digital equality needs understanding of why these disparities persist and leveraging digital technologies to remove or eliminate them. In this case in order to truly understand and achieve digital equality, we can’t just look at current technology access or future innovations. We also need to examine the historical roots of inequality that shape who gets left behind or left out “Digital equality is as much about digging into history as it is about making sense of the present, while planning for the future”(Sambuli N, 2019).

Relflection on Digital Equality and My opinion on the internet as a basic human right.

Week 10 Blog Post

Relflection on Digital Equality and My opinion on the internet as a basic human right.
Reatile Davhana
May , 2025

In the digital age, internet access is no longer a luxury but a vital human right, essential for equal participation in education, employment, and civic life; ensuring universal access and establishing democratic governance of the internet are critical steps toward addressing the deepening digital divide and promoting global equality

This reading focusses on how access to the internet is increasingly being viewed as a basic human right, this is presented mostly because of the Covid-19 pandemic. Drawing on ideas from Salvador Allende and recent decisions like that of the Indian Supreme Court, the text argues that lack of access to digital connectivity mirrors broader inequalities in capitalist societies. The pandemic was or has forced people to rely on the internet and digital infrastructure, deepening the divide between who can and cannot access education, employment and healthcare. The reliance on the internet and digital inequality now compounds existing social and economic disparities. The reading ends by raising two pressing questions, should internet access be universally provided, and who should govern the rules of the internet?

In my opinion, the internet should be a basic human right especially when it comes to how things are structured in the current economy. The internet is a primary gateway to knowledge, education and news. In an increasingly digital world, denying certain people internet can limit opportunities for learning and employment. People need to be economic and social participants, Jobs, banking healthcare and government services are increasingly moving online and digital, the lack of access to the internet can cause more inequality and marginalization in disadvantaged groups. When giving people access to the internet, it bridges geographical divides, allowing people to collaborate, share ideas, and maintain relationships across borders. he COVID-19 pandemic highlighted how essential internet access is for remote learning and work. Without it, many are left behind.

UN internet access human right 2016

The United Nations Human Rights Council officially passed a non-binding resolution that condemns intentional disruption of internet access and affirms that internet access is a fundamental human right. This resolution reaffirms that the same rights individuals possess offline such as freedom of expression and access to information must also be protected online. the resolution emphasizes that universal internet access plays a critical role in promoting economic development, supporting education, and safeguarding democratic participation. It urges states to bridge the digital divide by ensuring affordable, inclusive, and equitable internet availability for all, particularly for marginalized communities (UNHRC, 2016). In a digital age where access to information, online services, and remote communication is vital, the resolution's stance highlights the necessity of digital inclusion as a prerequisite for exercising broader human rights. By framing internet access as essential rather than optional, the UN emphasizes that a lack of connectivity can exacerbate social and economic inequalities, further marginalizing vulnerable populations.

These homegrown innovations prove Africa isn't just consuming technology but generating transformative digital approaches rooted in feminist principles and local contexts.

This linkage becomes evident when examining the type of content that circulates under the guise of humour, often at the expense of marginalized groups, reinforcing harmful stereotypes and power imbalances. The text convincingly argues that the internet is not an isolated space but rather an extension of existing societal hierarchies, meaning feminist resistance must address both digital and material realities.

The ease with which degrading content is amplified demonstrates how digital spaces can perpetuate and even intensify real-world oppression. This underscores the urgent need for feminist interventions both in challenging harmful online narratives and in dismantling the offline structures that enable them. While technology offers opportunities, it often excludes marginalized voices especially African women, queer communities, and rural populations. The COVID-19 pandemic exposed these gaps, showing how privilege determines who gets to participate online. However, African feminists are leading the charge for change by creating safer spaces, challenging biased algorithms, and demanding fair policies.

How does code matter in the context of decolonialism.

Week 12 Blog Post

How does code matter in the context of decolonialism.
Reatile Davhana
May , 2025

Code is not neutral. Every algorithm, dataset, and user interface embeds the biases and power structures of the people who create it often replicating colonial hierarchies under the guise of technological progress. There are things with design flaws like facial recognition systems that misidentify people that are of colour to the elimination of indigenous languages in natural language processing, technology is known and has continued to show the frequent reinforcements of power imbalances that decolonial movement seek to stop or dismantle. Even seemingly "apolitical" coding practices such as choosing which languages to support, whose problems to prioritize, or whose knowledge is deemed authoritative can perpetuate marginalization.

Programming languages and digital interfaces are deeply rooted in Western, Anglophone norms. Keywords in nearly all major programming languages such as if, else and while are based on English, creating an implicit barrier for non-native speakers. Similarly, Unicode the standard for text encoding prioritizes Latin scripts, while some Indigenous and minority scripts remain unsupported or poorly rendered.

Recently, I've implemented several important changes to my website's blog section to address usability issues that were affecting the reading experience. The blog posts were originally displayed in a tightly clustered layout, with m inimal spacing between articles. This dense arrangement created visual distortion and made it difficult for users to focus on individual posts, particularly when reading longer content. The lack of proper spacing between elements also led to inconsistent rendering across different devices and screen sizes.

To solve these problems, I developed a JavaScript solution that introduces interactive "Read More" buttons for each blog post. This implementation gives visitors much greater control over their reading experience. Now users can choose which articles they want to expand and read in full, while keeping others in a condensed preview state. This approach not only improves readability by reducing visual clutter, but also helps pages load faster since less content is rendered initially. The expandable sections feature smooth animations that make the interaction feel natural and intuitive.

Looking ahead, I'm planning two additional enhancements to further improve site navigation. First, I'll be adding a "Back to Top" button that will appear when users scroll down the page. This simple but effective feature will allow visitors to quickly return to the top of long pages without excessive scrolling, particularly valuable for mobile users and those reading lengthy articles. The button will be strategically positioned and designed to be noticeable without being obtrusive.

These improvements are part of my ongoing effort to create a website that prioritizes user experience and accessibility. By giving visitors more control over how they interact with content and navigate the site, I aim to create a more comfortable and efficient browsing experience for all users. Each change is being carefully implemented with attention to visual design, performance impact, and accessibility standards to ensure the best possible results.

The Ethical Internet: What It Should Look Like

Week 3 Blog Post

The Ethical Internet: What It Should Look Like?
Reatile Davhana
May , 2025

The internet is a huge part of our lives, but it's not always fair or safe. Right now, many websites and apps track what we do, keep us addicted, and sometimes spread lies or hate. An ethical internet would fix these problems by putting people first.

First, an ethical internet would respect our privacy. Too many companies collect our personal data what we search, where we go, even private messages and sell it to advertisers. It shouldn’t be this way. We should control our own information, with strong privacy settings and the right to delete our data if we want. Imagine if social media worked like a locked diary only you decide who sees it.

Second, apps and websites shouldn’t be designed to keep us hooked. Have you ever opened TikTok or YouTube "just for a minute" and wasted an hour? That’s not an accident. Companies use tricks like endless scrolling and autoplay to make us stay longer. An ethical internet would avoid these manipulative designs and even remind us to take breaks instead of trying to trap us online.

Third, the internet should be fair to everyone. Sometimes, algorithms (the code that decides what we see) are biased. Job ads might only go to certain people, or social media might push extreme content just because it gets clicks. An ethical internet would remove these unfair biases and be transparent about how things work. We should choose what we see, not have companies decide for us.

Fourth, fake news and hate speech need to stop. Lies spread fast online, and some people use the internet to bully others or spread dangerous ideas. An ethical internet would fact-check viral posts, remove harmful content, and teach people how to spot misinformation. The internet should help us find the truth, not drown us in lies.

Finally, the internet should be sustainable and available to all. Right now, data centres use tons of electricity, and not everyone can afford good internet access. An ethical internet would run on clean energy, be affordable, and work for people with disabilities like having screen readers for the blind.

We can all help make the internet better. Use apps that respect privacy, like Signal instead of WhatsApp. Be mindful of screen time, and don’t share posts unless you know they’re true. If we demand fairer rules and make smarter choices, we can create an internet that helps people instead of exploiting them. The internet should be a place for learning, creativity, and real connection not just profits and manipulation.
